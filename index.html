Her Immigration Story

My mother, Guadalupe DeNunez Galvan, was born in Michoacan, Mexico in 1971. She was raised within Cotija, a small city in Michoacan, with her 12 siblings. She was the 11th child in the family but she was never treated like the youngest. My grandfather would always make her do the same amount of work as her older sisters. She was expected to wake up at 4 in the morning to bring water for the rest of the family. She also needed to finish her other chores around the house before noon. She never did complain about the work or asked for a break. The only other thing that my mother enjoyed more than helping her family was dancing. She would go to every party she could and dance for the whole night. It was at one of these parties where she met my father. That night she was on the dance floor when she noticed my father sitting in a chair. She went up to him and asked if he would want to dance. My father, keeping his head down, said he did not know how to dance. My mother laughed to tears as she heard the response. But it was that response that made her fall in love with him. The following year she married him. In July 30, 1997, she gave birth to her first child, Antonio Nunez Galvan. However, the following 3 years Mexico were getting more and more unsafe. My parents decided it was time for them to travel to United States. The plan was to have my father go to the US first for work. As he is working, he would send money back for my mother. During his time in the US he would also try to get his citizenship. Once he is a citizen he would marry my mother in California so she would be able to cross the border without any harm. But this did not go as planned because my mother got pregnant with me.

Pregnant!

This picture was taken the same month my mom found out she was pregnant with me.

My father told my mother to just have the baby be born in Mexico since the laywer told him that he would get his citizenship within the next two years. However my mother did not want her second child to be born in an unsafe place. During the time my mother was pregnant with me, more and more people have been disappearing. When my mother was three months pregnant she took it upon herself to make the journey to the United States without telling my father. She knew she couldn't walk or swim a long distance because she was taking my older brother with her. So, she asked someone to take her across the border. The man she asked was known in the city as crazy, always talking to himself and screaming randomly. But my mother heard he used to smuggle people in the U.S. She told me that the man smelled like urinate and looked like he never took a shower but she still asked him if he would take her across the border. The man told my mother he would do it for a bottle tequila. That same day she packed two small bags and carried the bottle in one of her arms. In the other arms she held my older brother's hand and both of them walked to the plaza waiting for the man. The man arrived in a pick-up truck filled with cheese. He told my mother when they get close to the border she would need to hide inside the seats and not make sound. It took about 15 minutes for them to cross but those were the longest 15 minutes of my mother's life. Once they were at California my mother called my father and told him she was in LA. My father did admit to me that he was scared, confused, and happy all at once when he got the news. He went to LA and brought my mother and older brother to Santa Rosa where he was living in a one bedroom apartment. That same year on November 19, 1999 I was born in Santa Rosa, California. Two years later my father passed his citizenship test in February and in May he offically married my mother.

Arriving in the United States

My mother had my two younger brothers, David on April 23, 2002 and Ramon September 11, 2003. In 2006, my father was able to buy a house for us in Santa Rosa. That same year my mother started to work as a special ed teacher assistant. She also started to dance for the local church within our city. In 2016, was when she was told that she had cancer, a growing tumor in her left leg. This year was the hardest for her physically. She needed to do two surgeries to remove the tumor. After the surgeries, she needed radiation therapy to treat the remaining cancer. She did have some side effect from the treatment however the one that was the most difficult was the lack of ability to travel. She was not allowed to go to Mexico until she was fully healed. My mother was cancer free in 2018 but she still finds it difficult to walk and stand with her left leg. Nevertheless, she still continues to live her life even with the pain. She is a fighter. She is a person who will do anything for her family, not afraid of any obstacles that wait her. She is a migrate.
